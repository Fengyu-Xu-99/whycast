# Selected Path: Bloodhound Test

## Path Summary

This episode starts from a concrete game observation and follows one main question:

> Why does a scent-tracking dog look like this?

The answer should not be just "Bloodhounds have good noses." The episode should show how body, senses, behavior, and human-selected work fit together.

## Episode Path

1. **Trigger:** I noticed the Bloodhound in *The Hunter: Call of the Wild* because it looked strange and helped track blood trails.
2. **Body:** Bloodhound features are not random: long face, big nostrils, long ears, loose skin, heavy stable body.
3. **Smell World:** Dogs do not just smell one odor; they read traces, direction, freshness, interference, and environmental change.
4. **Tracking Skill:** Bloodhound is not simply "the best nose"; it is one of the classic specialists for long, persistent individual-scent tracking.
5. **Scent Hounds:** Beagle and Coonhound show how different scent-work problems create different bodies and behaviors.
6. **Sight Hounds:** Greyhound, Whippet, Saluki, and Borzoi show the opposite logic: visual lock-on, speed, and short pursuit windows.
7. **Main Takeaway:** Different hunting dogs are different answers to different tasks.

## Narrative Promise

By the end, the listener should feel:

- Bloodhound anatomy makes sense in its working context.
- "Good at smelling" is too vague; tracking is a whole-body system.
- Scent hounds and sight hounds reveal two different ways of solving hunting problems.
- Dog bodies are easier to understand when you ask what job the body is answering.
